Humid air and saturation

Scope: frozen composition, no phase change

IdealGasThermo.jl models frozen-composition ideal gases — there is no condensation, latent heat, or vapour–liquid equilibrium. "Humid air" here is dry air plus a fixed amount of water vapour; the saturation-pressure model only tells you when that vapour would begin to condense (the dew point), not what happens afterwards.

Moist air as a FrozenGas

humid_air builds dry air plus water vapour, specified by either the specific humidity $\omega$ (SH) or the relative humidity (RH):

using IdealGasThermo

wet = humid_air(RH = 0.60, T = 300.0, P = 101325.0)   # 60% RH at 27 °C
dry = FrozenGas(DryAir)

(R_wet = R(wet), R_dry = R(dry))
(R_wet = 289.2899943888362, R_dry = 287.0521912832069)

Water ($M \approx 18$ g/mol) is lighter than dry air ($\approx 29$ g/mol), so adding vapour raises the specific gas constant $R = \Ru/M$. The result is an ordinary FrozenGas, so all the usual accessors apply:

(cp = c_p(wet, 300.0), h = h(wet, 300.0))
(cp = 1015.8462600516335, h = -174568.3637412096)

Saturation and the dew point

The water saturation pressure $P_\text{sat}(T)$ sets the maximum vapour the air can hold; relative humidity is $\mathrm{RH} = p_v / P_\text{sat}(T)$:

T = 300.0
Psat = IdealGasThermo.saturation_vapor_pressure(T)   # Pa
3527.7707872046717

The specific humidity at saturation ($\mathrm{RH} = 1$) at this temperature and pressure is the most vapour the parcel could carry before condensing:

ω_sat = IdealGasThermo.specific_humidity(1.0, T, 101325.0)
0.021654566422826652

Cooling the 60%-RH parcel raises its relative humidity ($P_\text{sat}$ falls while $p_v$ is fixed) until it reaches $\mathrm{RH} = 1$ — the dew point. Below it, the frozen-composition model no longer applies, because water would leave the gas phase.
